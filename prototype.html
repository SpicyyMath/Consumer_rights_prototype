⏰ Time Limits

You have up to 6 years to make a claim for major failures, but it's best to act quickly. For minor problems, you should give the seller a reasonable opportunity to fix the issue first.

💡 Important Notes

Consumer guarantees apply regardless of any manufacturer's warranty. You don't need to accept store credit - you can insist on a refund for major failures.

What to do next:

  1. Gather your proof of purchase (receipt, bank statement, email confirmation).
  2. Contact the retailer where you bought the product.
  3. Calmly and clearly explain the problem and what you want (a refund, repair, or replacement).
  4. If they refuse, mention your rights under Australian Consumer Law. Use the script below as a guide.
  5. If there is still no resolution, you can contact your state's consumer protection agency (like Fair Trading or Consumer Affairs).

Know Your Rights

Under Australian Consumer Law, products you buy must meet certain automatic guarantees. Understanding these is the first step to solving a problem.

What is a "Major Failure"?

If a problem is major, you get to choose the remedy (refund, replacement, or repair).

What is a "Minor Failure"?

If a problem is minor, the store can choose to repair the item, replace it, or give you a refund.

Acceptable Quality

Products must be safe, durable, and free from defects, considering their price and type.

Proof of Purchase

You need to prove where and when you bought an item, but it doesn't have to be a paper receipt.

What to Say

Being prepared can make the conversation with a retailer much easier. Here are some scripts you can adapt.

1. For a Major Failure (Requesting a Refund)

"Hello, I purchased this [Product Name] on [Date]. Unfortunately, it has a major failure because it [e.g., 'won't turn on at all']. Under Australian Consumer Law, this entitles me to a remedy of my choice. I would like a full refund."

2. For a Minor Failure (Requesting a Repair)

"Hi, I bought this [Product Name] here on [Date]. It's not working correctly because [e.g., 'the battery only lasts for an hour']. Under my consumer guarantees, I'd like you to arrange a repair for the item within a reasonable time."

3. If the Retailer Refuses or Blames the Manufacturer

"I understand your position, but my rights under the Australian Consumer Law are with the seller of the product, which is your store. The problem is a breach of the consumer guarantees, and I need you to provide the remedy. I am not required to contact the manufacturer."

4. After a Repair Has Failed

"Hi, I brought this [Product Name] in for repair on [Date], but the same problem is still happening. Since the initial repair was unsuccessful, this is now considered a major failure. I would now like to request a full refund."

5. If a Repair is Taking Too Long

"Hello, I dropped off my [Product Name] for repair over [e.g., 'three weeks'] ago. A repair must be done within a reasonable time. This delay is too long, so I am now asking for a replacement product instead."

6. For an Item That Doesn't Match the Online Description

"I recently bought this [Product Name] from your website. The item I received does not match the description online because [e.g., 'it is a different colour and is missing the advertised camera feature']. This is a major failure, and I would like to return it for a full refund, including any shipping costs."

7. To Claim for Damage Caused by a Faulty Product (Consequential Loss)

"Hello, the [Faulty Product Name] I bought here malfunctioned and [e.g., 'leaked, damaging my carpet']. The fault with the product has caused this additional damage. Under consumer law, you are responsible for this consequential loss. I am seeking a refund for the product and compensation for the carpet cleaning bill."

8. When a Salesperson Pushes an 'Extended Warranty'

"Thank you for the information, but I don't wish to purchase an extended warranty. I'm aware that under Australian Consumer Law, I already have automatic guarantees that the product will be of acceptable quality and last a reasonable time, regardless of a manufacturer's warranty."

9. If a Product is Unsafe

"Hi, I'm returning this [Product Name] I bought on [Date]. It is unsafe because it [e.g., 'overheats to the point where it's too hot to touch']. This is a major failure under consumer law, and I require an immediate full refund."

10. For a Faulty Digital Product or Subscription

"Hello, I purchased a 12-month subscription to [Software/Service Name] on [Date]. A key feature, [e.g., 'the cloud backup'], has stopped working, making the service unusable for me. Since this is a major failure of the service, I'd like to cancel my subscription and receive a refund for the remaining unused portion."

Case Studies

See how consumer law applies in real situations.

1. The Smartphone That Died at 13 Months

Situation: Sarah's expensive smartphone stopped working 13 months after she bought it. The store said it was out of its 12-month warranty.

Outcome: Sarah explained that a reasonable person would expect an expensive phone to last longer than 13 months. This is a breach of the 'acceptable quality' guarantee. The store agreed to provide a free repair.

2. The 'Smart' TV Missing Apps

Situation: Tom bought a Smart TV advertised as having all major streaming apps. He discovered it was missing a key app.

Outcome: The TV did not match its description. This is a major failure. Tom was entitled to choose his remedy and requested a full refund.

3. The Drifting Game Controller

Situation: Six months after purchase, a game controller's analog stick started 'drifting', making games unplayable.

Outcome: The product had a defect and was not of acceptable quality. This was a minor failure, and the store chose to provide a free replacement controller.

4. The Laptop with a Faulty Graphics Card

Situation: Eight months after buying a high-end laptop for graphic design, the graphics card failed, making it useless for his work.

Outcome: This is a major failure because the laptop could no longer be used for its normal (and advertised) purpose. He was entitled to reject the laptop and chose a full refund over a repair.

5. The Noise-Cancelling Headphones

Situation: Aisha bought premium noise-cancelling headphones. After four months, the noise-cancellation feature stopped working, though audio still played.

Outcome: This is a minor failure. The product was still usable as standard headphones, but a key feature was broken. The store was obligated to offer a free repair to restore the function.

6. The Tablet with Poor Battery Life

Situation: A tablet was advertised with an "all-day 10-hour battery". In reality, it only lasted 3 hours, even with light use.

Outcome: The product did not match the specific claims made about its performance. This is a major failure, and the customer was entitled to a refund.

7. The Refurbished Phone

Situation: Ben bought a refurbished phone sold "as is" with a 30-day store warranty. It failed completely on day 45.

Outcome: The "as is" term does not remove consumer guarantees. A reasonable person would expect a refurbished phone to last longer than 45 days. Ben was entitled to a remedy (likely a repair or replacement) from the seller.

8. The Smart Watch Causing a Rash

Situation: Chloe developed a significant skin rash from the band of her new smartwatch. The product was unsafe for her.

Outcome: Because the product was unsafe, it was a major failure. Chloe was entitled to a full refund.

9. The Drone That Fell from the Sky

Situation: Leo's new drone suffered a software glitch mid-flight and crashed, breaking into pieces.

Outcome: The drone was not free from defects and was not fit for purpose. As it could not be easily and quickly fixed, it was a major failure, entitling Leo to a replacement or refund.

10. The Online Software That Didn't Work

Situation: A company bought a yearly subscription for a digital design tool, but it was incompatible with their operating system, despite the website not mentioning any restrictions.

Outcome: The digital product was not fit for the purpose the business made known to the supplier (by virtue of it being a standard operating system). This was a major failure, and the company was entitled to cancel the contract and get a refund.

What is a "Major Failure"?

A product or service has a major failure when it has a problem that would have stopped someone from buying it if they’d known about it. If the failure is major, you have the right to choose your preferred remedy: a full refund, a replacement, or a repair.

It is considered a major failure when the product:

  • Is significantly different from the sample or description. For example, you order a blue laptop online, but a black one is delivered.
  • Is substantially unfit for its common purpose and can’t easily be fixed within a reasonable time. For example, a new smartphone that constantly drops calls.
  • Is unsafe. For example, an electronic device that gives you a small electric shock when you plug it in.
  • Is unfit for a purpose you specifically told the seller about. For example, you tell a salesperson you need a computer for high-end video editing, but the one they sell you constantly crashes when using editing software.
  • Has a defect that would have stopped you from buying it had you known. For example, a gaming console that has a widely known hardware fault that makes it unplayable.

In these situations, the choice of remedy is yours. The retailer cannot insist on repairing the item if you would prefer a refund or replacement.

What is a "Minor Failure"?

A minor failure is any problem with a product or service that is not a major failure. It's a problem that can be fixed in a reasonable amount of time.

If your product has a minor failure, the store or seller gets to choose the remedy. They can offer you a:

  • Repair: The store must arrange for the item to be repaired at no cost to you and within a reasonable time.
  • Replacement: The store can offer you a replacement item that is identical or of similar value.
  • Refund: The store can offer to give your money back.

What if the store fails to act?

If the store fails to provide a remedy within a reasonable time, or refuses to provide one at all, you can:

  1. Get the item repaired elsewhere and claim the reasonable costs back from the original store.
  2. Reject the product and ask for a refund or replacement. At this point, the problem is treated like a major failure.

"Reasonable time" isn't defined in law and depends on the product and the problem, but you shouldn't have to wait weeks for a simple fix.

Acceptable Quality

The consumer guarantee of "acceptable quality" is one of the most important rights you have. It means goods must be as:

  • Fit for all the purposes for which goods of that kind are commonly supplied. A laptop should be able to browse the internet; a smartphone should make calls.
  • Acceptable in appearance and finish. A new device shouldn't have scratches or dents.
  • Free from defects. A product shouldn't have faults that prevent it from working correctly.
  • Safe. It must not pose a safety risk to the user.
  • Durable. It should last for a reasonable amount of time.

What is "reasonable"?

The test for acceptable quality is what a "reasonable consumer" would expect, fully aware of the product's condition at the time of sale. This depends on factors like:

  • Price: You would expect a $2000 laptop to be more durable and have a better finish than a $300 one.
  • Description: How the product was described on its packaging, on the label, or by the salesperson.
  • Any other representations made about the goods.

This guarantee applies even after any manufacturer's warranty has expired. A one-year warranty does not mean the product only needs to last for one year if a reasonable consumer would expect it to last longer.

Proof of Purchase

To exercise your consumer rights, you need to be able to show that you bought the item from that specific seller. This is called providing proof of purchase.

While a tax invoice or receipt is the best proof, it is not the only kind you can use. Other common forms of proof of purchase include:

  • A credit or debit card statement
  • A confirmation email or number for an internet or phone transaction
  • A warranty card showing the date and place of purchase
  • A lay-by agreement
  • A serial or production number, if it can be linked to the sale in the seller's system

Do I need the original box?

No, a retailer cannot refuse to provide a remedy simply because you no longer have the original packaging. Your rights apply whether you have the box or not.
